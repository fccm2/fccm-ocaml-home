cosinus-and-sinus-tutorial

cosinus and sinus, are functions that permit you to get x and y coordinates, of points on a circle.

You can get these coordinates, based on an angle.

The math area for these kind of functions and its coordinate-system is called: "trigonometry" (or "trigo" for short.)

cosinus and sinus functions return a float that is between [0.0 .. 1.0], or [0.0 .. -1.0] for angles that are in the negative area of the axis.

The range [0.0 .. 1.0], is for a circle which radius is equal to the unit (radius: r=1).

For a radius that is r=3.6, the values that are in the range [0.0 .. 1.0], just have to be multiplied by the value of the radius:

x=(r*(cos a))
y=(r*(sin a))
p=(x,y)

These coordinates correspond to a circle of radius: (r), but for a circle that is still centered at the origin of the x and y -axis.

x=cx+(r*(cos a))
y=cy+(r*(sin a))
p=(x,y)

For a circle that is centered at (cx,cy), we just have to add (cx) and (cy), to the (x) and (y) coordinates. This is a kind of "translation", it "translate" a circle centered at the origine, to a circle that can be positioned anywhere in the area, with a center which is not the origine O w/ coords (0,0), but another point, the center of the circle C, with coords: (cx,cy).

The multiplication by the radius has to be operated first, because the cos and sin functions are giving result for a unit-circle, so proportionally we have to multiply first to create coordinates of a point on a biger circle, before to apply the translation.

Here if the angle a is 30.0 degrees, we first have to convert it in radians, w/:

a=((a / 180.0) * PI)

where pi is probably something like 3.14159265358979312.

we divide by 180.0 which is half of a circle-angle, in degrees, and then multiply by pi which is half of a circle, in radian.

we need to convert in radian's, because all the trigo function are made to return their result with angles expressed in radian's.
